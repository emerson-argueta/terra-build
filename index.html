The Problem
An Industry Protected
From Competition

Building a standard home in California costs $300–$500 per square foot, before land. That number isn't driven by physics or materials — it's driven by an industry protected by favorable regulations, lobbying, and the absence of real alternatives.

Adobe — compressed earth construction — has been used in earthquake-prone regions for thousands of years. Modern engineering has made it dramatically safer and more consistent. It is cheaper, greener, and thermally superior to conventional construction.

It is effectively banned in California. Not because it doesn't work. Because the code system that would certify it is dominated by the industry that profits from keeping it out.

The barriers to adobe in California are regulatory and political — not physical or scientific. The engineering works. What's missing is the integrated system that makes it buildable, certifiable, and impossible to ignore.

How It Works
From Dirt to Certified Block
in Under a Week
Manufacturing

The Mobile Production Unit

A single trailer brings a complete block-making factory to your site. It tests the soil already there, adds a precisely formulated amendment package — basalt fiber, natural latex, mineral correctors — and compresses the mix into interlocking certified blocks at 300–500 per hour. Every block is stamped with a QR code containing its complete production record. A full house worth of blocks in 3–7 days of production.

Software

Location-Specific Design

California earthquakes are not all the same. Each location has a unique seismic signature — frequency, pulse type, soil amplification, fault directionality. The USGS has mapped all of this precisely. Terra Build's software translates that data into geometric building rules: exact wall thickness, corner geometry, buttress spacing, and opening sizes for that specific site. Enter an address. Get a buildable certified plan.

Wall System

Engineered for Seismic and Thermal Performance

The wall is a layered system: a dense thermal core of pure adobe for maximum heat buffering, outer layers of fiber-rich adobe for seismic ductility, latex-modified mortar joints that absorb energy at every course, and an external basalt fiber mesh that holds cracked walls standing. Thermal performance preserved. Seismic performance dramatically improved over traditional adobe.

Policy

Building the Case for Code Recognition

Codes follow political reality — they don't lead it. The strategy starts in rural California counties with simpler permitting, builds peer-reviewed research through UC Berkeley and Peru's leading earthquake lab, secures surplus-lines insurance, and frames the political case as a housing affordability issue. The cob construction movement achieved IRC code recognition in 2021 after a decade of organized effort. This is the same playbook, better resourced.

The Model
Structured to Stay
Affordable Forever

Terra Build is not structured to make investors rich. It is structured to make the status quo economically impossible. Every design choice in the organizational model exists to prevent cost inflation from creeping back in.

Software

Free to Owner-Builders

The software core is open source and free to anyone building their own home. Small commercial licenses for builders fund ongoing development — not profit extraction from the people who need it most.

Manufacturing

Cooperative Ownership

MPUs are owned by regional cooperatives — like agricultural equipment cooperatives that have let farmers share expensive machinery at cost for a century. No investor return requirement embedded in every rental rate.

Materials

Cost-Based Pricing

Amendment packages — the consumable at the heart of the system — are priced at cost plus operating margin. A fraction of equivalent conventional material costs. Always.

Research

Independent Evidence

A nonprofit research entity partnered with UC system and PUCP produces the peer-reviewed evidence base. Funded by HCD grants and foundations — not by anyone with a financial stake in the outcome.

The Bottom Line
What Happens If This Works

A California family that today cannot qualify for a mortgage on a $600,000+ home — the current floor for most new construction — could potentially build on purchased land for $190,000–$240,000 in total construction cost at full system operation.

That's not just cheaper. That's a different economic category entirely. The difference between homeownership being inaccessible and being achievable for middle-income California families.

Conventional builders either adopt the Terra Build system or become uncompetitive on cost. Insurance and code bodies face political pressure to recognize a system that solves housing affordability. The industry adapts or exits.
